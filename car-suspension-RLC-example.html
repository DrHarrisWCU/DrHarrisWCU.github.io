Car suspension vs. parallel RLC (step response simulator)

Left-click adds or removes a step (the road permanently changes level). Right-click toggles a repeating series of bumps, so you can see whether the suspension keeps up or falls behind. Synced live to the matching parallel RLC voltage.

Left-click: add/remove a plateau. Right-click: start or stop a repeating series of bumps.

Car suspension: mass should track the wheel once any bounce settles

Parallel RLC: capacitor voltage, reacts to each step

A step is a step response: the road permanently moves to a new level, like a source turning on and staying on, so the system settles at a new final value. Left-click an already-stepped section to bring it back down to ground level. Right-click starts a steady stream of small bumps for the underdamped and critically damped cases, so you can see whether each new disturbance arrives before the last one has settled. The mass height plays the role of capacitor voltage; the shock absorber's resistance plays the role of R.

Right-click also works for the overdamped case now: each bump produces the same slow, sluggish, non-oscillating creep as a single step, so a fast train of bumps tends to blur together into a smoother-looking ride in this simple linear model — even though watching for the brief shake and flash right at each contact point still shows the shock absorber's force spiking sharply and quickly, which is the actual jarring sensation a rider would feel.
